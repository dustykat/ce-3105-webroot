The primary purpose of this fluids laboratory course is not simply to repeat known formulas or perform routine measurements—it is to test, calibrate, and validate models of fluid behavior through structured experimentation.

Engineering relies on models to represent how the physical world behaves under various conditions. These models may be derived from physical laws (e.g., conservation of mass or energy), empirical observations, or simplifications of complex phenomena.

In this course, we will follow a consistent experimental process rooted in the scientific method:

  1. Review the governing theory.
    Identify the underlying model for the system under investigation—whether it’s Bernoulli’s equation, the Darcy-Weisbach equation, or a momentum balance.

  2. Predict behavior based on the model.
    Before touching any equipment, calculate what should happen based on the inputs and assumptions. This includes identifying dependent variables, known parameters, and expected relationships.

  3. Measure the system’s behavior experimentally.
    Use lab equipment to collect data. Take care to understand how instruments work, and estimate uncertainty where appropriate.

  4. Compare model predictions with measurements.
    Plot your results, compute error metrics (such as percent error, root-mean-square error, or residuals), and assess whether the model agrees with reality.

  5. Evaluate and reflect.
    Consider whether deviations are due to limitations in the model, instrumentation error, procedural issues, or real-world complexity. Can the model be recalibrated? Was a core assumption invalid?

Each lab will begin with a Mini-Model Statement in the pre-lab, and will conclude with a structured comparison between theory and observation. Your task is not simply to “verify” formulas—but to explore how models perform in real-world conditions.

This reflective, model-driven approach is central to the work of engineers.

The Role of Engineering Experimentation#

The primary role of engineering experimentation is to progress from theory (dreamed up in our biological computers) to validation (confirm our theory is useful, and how accurate a predictor it may be).

Learning Objectives#

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  • Explain the scientific method in historical and engineering contexts

  • Distinguish between physical laws, empirical models, and measured data

  • Apply model-based reasoning to experimental design

  • Use error analysis to evaluate the agreement between theory and experiment

Part 1: The Scientific Method — Historical and Practical#

In this laboratory, we apply the scientific method as a structured process for testing and validating models of fluid behavior. Each experiment will follow a five-step workflow that mirrors this approach:

Theory Review – We begin by identifying the governing physical principles and equations that define the theoretical model (e.g., Bernoulli’s equation, momentum balance, or head loss formulas).

Model Prediction – Using known parameters and appropriate assumptions, we calculate what the model predicts should occur under the given experimental conditions.

Measurement and Observation – We conduct the experiment carefully, collecting data through direct measurement of key variables such as flow rate, pressure, or velocity.

Comparison and Error Analysis – We analyze how well the measured values align with the predicted results, using plots and error metrics such as percent error or RMS error to quantify agreement.

Interpretation and Evaluation – Finally, we reflect on the model’s performance. Where results diverge from theory, we consider the influence of experimental error, unmodeled effects, or invalid assumptions, and discuss whether the model should be accepted, rejected, or refined.

This cycle turns each experiment into a rigorous test of theory, helping you build both conceptual understanding and practical insight into fluid mechanics.

A Brief History#

Wikipedia: History of the Scientific Method presents a good discourse on the history of the “method;” notable parts of that history include:

  • Francis Bacon (1620s): Proposed structured observation and inductive reasoning. The Baconian method is the investigative method developed by Francis Bacon, one of the founders of modern science, and thus a first formulation of a modern scientific method. The method was put forward in Bacon’s book Novum Organum (1620), or ‘New Method’, to replace the old methods put forward in Aristotle’s Organon. It influenced the early modern rejection of medieval Aristotelianism. He explicitly addressed causality in his writings on the subject.

  • Galileo Galilei: Used mathematical models to describe physical phenomena, and designed experiments to test them. Galileo Galilei played a pivotal role in shaping the modern scientific method by emphasizing experimentation, observation, and mathematical analysis. He developed a method that involved intuition, demonstration, and experimental verification to test hypotheses and build upon existing knowledge. Galileo’s work, particularly in physics and astronomy, demonstrated the power of combining observation, experimentation, and mathematical reasoning to understand the natural world

  • Isaac Newton: Developed universal laws that combined observation, theory, and prediction. Isaac Newton’s scientific method was a refined approach to scientific inquiry that emphasized observation, experimentation, and the use of mathematical reasoning to develop and test theories. He used a combination of theoretical reasoning and empirical observation to draw inferences and develop his groundbreaking theories of motion and gravitation.

Engineering Adaptation#

The engineering design process is a structured approach to solving problems, building upon the principles of the scientific method but with a focus on practical application and creating tangible solutions. The primary tool is a model which relates the excitation-response behavior of the world. Design can rely upon the model once it is demonstrated to be reliable and accurate - the point of experimentation is usually to test the model (for applicability, reliability, and accuracy).

  • Theories (e.g., conservation laws) → produce models

  • Models → generate predictions given specific inputs

  • Experiments → gather data to test how well predictions match reality

Part 2: Reinforcement Activities#

Activity 1: Matching Models to Measurements#

Students are given:

  • 3 theoretical models:

    • Flow through an orifice

    • Pipe friction loss

    • Jet impact momentum

  • 3 sets of plotted or tabulated measurements

Task: Match each dataset to a model and identify likely causes of mismatch.

Activity 2 (Optional): Quick Error Propagation Exercise#

Given:

  • Model: \(Q = C_d A \sqrt{2gh} \)

  • Variables with uncertainties:

    • \(h = 0.75 \pm 0.01 \ \text{m}\)

    • \(A = 0.0025 \pm 0.0001 \ \text{m}^2\)

    • \( C_d = 0.62 \pm 0.02\)

Task: Compute \(Q\) and its uncertainty via propagation, compare with observed spread in measurements.

Part 3: Framework for Fluids Laboratory#

Every experiment in this course will follow a consistent model-testing cycle based on the scientific method.

Experimental Workflow:#

  1. Theory Review

    • Identify governing laws or assumptions

    • Examples: Bernoulli’s equation, momentum balance, continuity

  2. Model Prediction

    • Use the theoretical model to compute expected behavior

    • Ex: Predict pressure drop, velocity, force, etc.

  3. Measurement & Observation

    • Take experimental data using instruments and sensors

    • Document uncertainties

  4. Comparison & Validation

    • Plot: experimental vs. model values

    • Compute error metrics:

      • Percent error

      • Root-mean-square error (RMSE)

      • Residuals or correlation coefficient

  5. Conclusion

    • Does the model accurately predict behavior?

    • If not, why? Assumption failure or measurement limits?

Reinforcement Assignments#

Pre-Lab: Mini-Model Statement#

Before each lab, students submit:

  • A short statement of the governing model

  • A prediction of expected outcome

  • List of assumptions and what would indicate “good agreement”

Post-Lab: Comparison and Reflection#

Lab reports must include:

  • Explicit comparison between predicted and measured values

  • At least one error analysis method

  • Final reflection: Does the experiment support the model? Why or why not?

Closing Thoughts#

Engineering is not just about applying formulas—it’s about testing ideas against reality. The lab is not a place to “get the right answer”—it’s a place to confront models with nature, and decide whether we believe our assumptions.

Structured Inquiry vs. “Cook-and-Look”: Two Paths Through the Laboratory#

Overview#

Not all experiments are created equal. In engineering education, two styles of experimentation commonly emerge:

  • Scientific-method-directed experiments, where theory guides the inquiry, predictions are made, and results are critically evaluated to test a hypothesis or validate a model.

  • “Cook-and-look” experiments, where a procedure is followed step-by-step with little theoretical framing or expectation, often leading to passive observation rather than active discovery.

Understanding the distinction is essential not only for academic success, but for cultivating the mindset of a practicing engineer.

Scientific-Method-Directed Experimentation#

Characteristics:#

  • Begins with a hypothesis or model derived from theory

  • Predictions are made in advance

  • Experiments are designed to test, calibrate, or refute the model

  • Measurements are compared to expectations with quantitative rigor

  • Discrepancies lead to refinement of models and deeper understanding

Notable Successes#

  • Galileo’s inclined plane experiments (1600s)
    Tested mathematical relationships between acceleration, time, and distance, providing early evidence for the laws of motion.

  • Cavendish’s measurement of the gravitational constant (1797)
    Designed to quantify Newton’s law of universal gravitation—not merely observe attraction.

  • Prandtl’s boundary layer experiments (early 1900s)
    Theoretical analysis led to the design of experiments that revealed the structure of viscous flow, revolutionizing fluid mechanics.

Notable Failures#

  • Michelson–Morley experiment (1887)
    Failed to detect the “aether wind” predicted by classical physics. Though it produced a “null result,” it motivated a shift in understanding that led to Einstein’s theory of relativity.

“Cook-and-Look” Experimentation#

Characteristics:#

  • Follows a fixed set of steps like a recipe, often from a lab manual

  • No hypothesis or model-driven prediction is made

  • Data is collected without clear expectations

  • Emphasis is on procedure compliance, not interpretation

  • Often ends with, “The results didn’t match, but we followed the steps.”

Occasional Successes#

  • Alexander Fleming’s discovery of penicillin (1928)
    Accidental contamination of a petri dish revealed antibacterial effects—not predicted by theory, but recognized through observation.

  • Discovery of cosmic microwave background (Penzias and Wilson, 1965)
    Researchers were troubleshooting antenna noise and stumbled onto the remnant of the Big Bang—without initially understanding the implications.

These examples show that “cook-and-look” can occasionally lead to breakthroughs, but only if the observer is prepared to recognize the unexpected and reframe the context.

Common Failures#

  • Countless teaching, research, and industrial laboratories where results are recorded without interpretation.

  • Industrial settings where procedures are followed, but underlying phenomena are misunderstood, leading to poor troubleshooting or design decisions

Why Scientific Method Matters in Engineering#

Engineers do not merely measure—they model, predict, and verify. Experiments are not recipes; they are tools to challenge assumptions, test applicability, and build confidence in our understanding of physical systems.

In this fluids laboratory, we reject the “cook-and-look” mindset. Instead, we adopt a model-driven approach that values prediction, observation, comparison, and critical thinking. Your role is not to confirm the manual—it is to test nature and decide whether our models are worthy of your trust.

Mini-Model Statement (Pre-Lab)#

Before performing the experiment, complete the following:

1. Governing Model or Principle#

Briefly describe the physical law or empirical relationship being tested.

Example: “This experiment is based on the Bernoulli equation, which relates pressure, velocity, and elevation along a streamline for inviscid flow.”

2. Model Equation(s)#

State the key equation(s) to be used for prediction, including definitions of all variables.

Example: \( p + \frac{1}{2} \rho V^2 + \rho g h = \text{constant}\)

3. Assumptions#

List the assumptions necessary for the model to apply.

Examples:

  • Steady flow

  • Incompressible fluid

  • Negligible viscous losses

4. Prediction of Behavior#

Based on the model and inputs, describe the expected behavior or result.

Example: “We expect that increasing the velocity will decrease the pressure at the nozzle exit due to energy conservation.”

5. Evaluation Criteria#

State how agreement between theory and experiment will be assessed.

Example: “Agreement will be evaluated using percent error between the predicted and measured velocities. A deviation under 10% will be considered acceptable.”

Post-Lab Reflection Rubric#

Include a structured reflection as the final section of your lab report. Address each of the following categories. This reflection is worth 15 points and should demonstrate your ability to connect theory with observation.

1. Agreement with the Model (5 points)#

  • Clearly describe whether your measurements aligned with the model’s predictions.

  • Use graphs, tables, or error metrics (e.g., % error, RMSE) to support your conclusion.

Example: “The measured head loss was within 8% of the predicted value for all trials, suggesting good agreement with the Darcy-Weisbach model.”

2. Explanation of Discrepancies (5 points)#

  • If results deviated from the model, identify possible reasons:

    • Instrument error

    • Violation of assumptions

    • Model oversimplification

  • Distinguish between random and systematic error where appropriate.

Example: “The persistent underestimation of force may be due to unaccounted friction in the pulley system, violating the ideal assumption of lossless transfer.”

3. Implications and Next Steps (5 points)#

  • Reflect on the experiment’s value in validating, challenging, or refining the model.

  • Suggest follow-up measurements, improved procedures, or potential calibrations.

Example: “This experiment suggests the need to include a correction factor for contraction losses when modeling short orifices under real-world conditions.”

Reflection Format Tips#

  • Use short paragraphs.

  • Refer to specific results or figures in your report.

  • Avoid vague conclusions like “It worked” or “It didn’t work.”

Total: 15 points
Scoring rubric will assess completeness, clarity, and thoughtfulness.

Example 1: Flow Measurement with a Venturi#

🔬 Part I — Mini-Model Statement (Pre-Lab)#

1. Governing Model or Principle#

This experiment is based on the Bernoulli equation, which states that for an incompressible, steady, inviscid flow along a streamline, the total mechanical energy is conserved. The pressure drop between the wide and narrow sections of a Venturi meter can be related to the flow velocity.

2. Model Equation(s)#

\(\Delta p = \frac{1}{2} \rho \left( V_2^2 - V_1^2 \right) \)

Where:

  • \(\Delta p\) = pressure difference between upstream and throat

  • \(\rho\) = fluid density

  • \(V_1, V_2\) = velocities at inlet and throat (related via continuity)

From continuity: \(Q = A_1 V_1 = A_2 V_2\)

3. Assumptions#

  • Steady flow

  • Incompressible fluid (water)

  • No energy loss due to friction or turbulence

  • Accurate pressure measurements via manometer

4. Prediction of Behavior#

We expect the pressure at the throat to be lower than at the inlet due to increased velocity. Using measured pressure drop and known cross-sectional areas, we can calculate discharge \(Q\) and compare it to direct volumetric measurements.

5. Evaluation Criteria#

We will compare the discharge calculated from the Bernoulli-based model to the flow rate measured by time-to-fill a graduated tank. Agreement within ±5% will be considered acceptable.

Part II — Post-Lab Reflection#

1. Agreement with the Model (5 pts)#

The model-based flow rate was within 4.2% of the directly measured volumetric flow rate for all trials. A comparison plot (Figure 3) shows a strong correlation (R² = 0.993) between predicted and measured values.

2. Explanation of Discrepancies (5 pts)#

Minor discrepancies likely arose from:

  • Inexact manometer readings due to parallax

  • Slight turbulence or energy loss not accounted for in the model

  • Assumption of ideal flow conditions may not be fully valid in the experimental apparatus

3. Implications and Next Steps (5 pts)#

This experiment confirms that the Bernoulli-based model provides a valid estimate of flow rate in a Venturi meter under controlled conditions. Future experiments could investigate how accuracy degrades with increasing turbulence or cavitation, or apply a discharge coefficient \( C_d\) to improve model fit.

Total: 15/15

🧪 Pre/Post-Lab Worksheet: Model-Based Experimentation#

🔬 Part I — Mini-Model Statement (Pre-Lab)#

1. Governing Model or Principle#

This experiment tests the empirical model for head loss due to local disturbances in pipe systems (elbows, valves, contractions). The model is based on energy loss per unit weight using the minor loss coefficient \( K\).

2. Model Equation(s)#

\( h_L = K \cdot \frac{V^2}{2g} \)

Where:

  • \( h_L\) = head loss across fitting

  • \( K\) = loss coefficient (dimensionless, provided in literature)

  • \( V\) = velocity through fitting

  • \( g\) = gravitational acceleration

3. Assumptions#

  • Steady, incompressible flow

  • Fully developed velocity profile

  • Fittings are ideal representations (sharp angles, no roughness)

  • Flow velocity is uniform and measured accurately

4. Prediction of Behavior#

Using literature \( K\) values and measured velocities, we will predict \( h_L\) and compare it to observed pressure drops measured with manometers. Agreement within ±10% is expected.

5. Evaluation Criteria#

We will calculate the theoretical \( h_L\) and compare it to measured values. Discrepancies will be assessed using percent error and qualitative examination of flow regimes (e.g., cavitation, swirl).

📊 Part II — Post-Lab Reflection#

1. Agreement with the Model (5 pts)#

Results varied significantly depending on the fitting. For a standard elbow, predicted and measured losses were within 7%, but for a sudden contraction, the measured loss was nearly 40% higher than predicted. The straight gate valve also showed large scatter.

2. Explanation of Discrepancies (5 pts)#

Discrepancies are likely due to:

  • Uncertainty in exact geometry and roughness of fittings vs. textbook assumptions

  • Unsteady flow and local turbulence not accounted for by static \( K\) values

  • Measurement error from manometers in low-head-loss scenarios

  • Flow separation or secondary flows in the contraction that violate assumed velocity profiles

3. Implications and Next Steps (5 pts)#

This experiment demonstrates that empirical \( K\) values may not generalize well without calibration. Real-world systems likely require field measurement or correction factors. Future work should include:

  • Repetition with digital pressure sensors

  • Calibration of system-specific \( K\) values using best-fit curves

  • CFD modeling of complex flow structures in contractions

Total: 15/15
