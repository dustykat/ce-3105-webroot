Investigation of Friction Losses in a Pipe

Authors:#

P.N. Guinn, and P. Olar Bear

Purpose#

The objective of this experiment is to investigate frictional loss with varying velocity and Reynolds number in a pipeline and to determine minor losses in pipe fittings, including bends, sudden expansions, and sudden contractions. Specifically, the experiment aims to:

  • Measure pressure drops across two locations on a pipeline for varying flow rates

  • Measure and compare pressure drops across different fittings to determine their respective loss coefficients (\(K\)).

  • Evaluate the relationship between friction factor and Reynolds number in a small pipeline.

  • Develop hands-on experience with data acquisition techniques, including manual flow rate measurements and the use of a Raspberry Pi-based flowmeter.

  • Validate experimental findings by comparing measured loss coefficients to standard tabulated values reported in the literature.

  • Encourage critical thinking by requiring:

    1. Explaination of probable sources of experimental error

    2. Using visualization to support findings and condense tabular results

Experimental Setup and Methods#

The two experimental configurations (pipe loss and fitting loss) are described in the sub-sections that follow.

Results and Discussion#

Data Analysis - Pipe Loss#

The manometer and handheld pressure meter readings closely matched, serving as reliable “ground truth.” The calculations used for computing friction factor were based on the manometer readings. Plots the observed (measured) friction factor versus Reynolds’ number, and theoretical friction factor versus Reynolds’ number are shown below - in laminar flow we anticipate the relationship to be \(f = \frac{64}{Re}\), so a log-log plot should appear as a straight line.

The script and plot below visually compares the observed and theoretical friction factors for the experimental conditions.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# ----------------------------
# Data
# ----------------------------
RE = np.array([573, 845, 1437, 1953, 2063])
# FF = theoretical; FF2 = measured (fill in your measured values)
FF  = np.array([0.111, 0.075, 0.044, 0.033, 0.031])   # theoretical (~64/RE)
FF2 = np.array([0.083 , 0.062 , 0.039 , 0.041 , 0.036])   # <<< lab measured/computed

# ----------------------------
# Prototype Functions
# ----------------------------
def fit_powerlaw(x_val, y_val):
    """
    Fit y = a * x^b via log-log linear regression.
    Returns: a, b, r2, y_pred
    """
    x = np.log(x_val)
    y = np.log(y_val)
    b, ln_a = np.polyfit(x, y, 1)
    a = np.exp(ln_a)

    y_pred = a * x_val**b
    ss_res = np.sum((y_val - y_pred)**2)
    ss_tot = np.sum((y_val - y_val.mean())**2)
    r2 = 1 - ss_res/ss_tot
    return a, b, r2, y_pred

# ----------------------------
# Fits
# ----------------------------
a1, b1, r2_1, FF_pred  = fit_powerlaw(RE, FF)
a2, b2, r2_2, FF2_pred = fit_powerlaw(RE, FF2)

# Smooth curve for plotting
RE_fit = np.linspace(RE.min(), RE.max(), 400)
FF_fit  = a1 * RE_fit**b1
FF2_fit = a2 * RE_fit**b2

# Equation strings
eqn1 = f"FF(th)  = {a1:.4f}·Re^{b1:.4f}   R²={r2_1:.4f}"
eqn2 = f"FF2(meas)= {a2:.4f}·Re^{b2:.4f}   R²={r2_2:.4f}"

print(eqn1)
print(eqn2)

# ----------------------------
# Plot (log–log)
# ----------------------------
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(8, 5))

# Data
ax.plot(RE, FF,  'o',  label='Theoretical (samples)', alpha=0.9)
ax.plot(RE, FF2, 's',  label='Measured (lab)', alpha=0.9)

# Fits
ax.plot(RE_fit, FF_fit,  '-',  label='Fit (theoretical)')
ax.plot(RE_fit, FF2_fit, '--', label='Fit (measured)')

# Axes scaling and limits
ax.set_xscale('log')
ax.set_yscale('log')
ax.set_xlim(500, 3000)
ax.set_ylim(0.03, 0.15)

# Labels, grid, legend
ax.set_xlabel('Reynolds Number (log scale)')
ax.set_ylabel('Friction Factor (log scale)')
ax.set_title('Friction Factor vs. Reynolds Number — Theoretical vs Measured (Power-Law Fits)')
ax.grid(True, which='both', linestyle='--', alpha=0.6)
ax.legend()

# Annotation
ax.text(
    0.02, 0.24,
    eqn1 + "\n" + eqn2,
    transform=ax.transAxes, va='top', ha='left',
    bbox=dict(boxstyle='round', facecolor='white', alpha=0.8)
)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

# (Optional) If you want to overlay the canonical laminar theory f=64/Re:

Interpretation

The experimental results display the correct sign on the fitted exponent in agreement with laminar flow theory. The magnitude is such that the plotted line has a lower slope than anticipated. The constant is 16 times too small, suggesting considerable experimental error.

  1. Reading uncertainty on the manometer – the smallest division is 2 mm, which limits resolution. Readings that are too low would underpredict head loss and can explain the low friction factor values as compared to theoretical values.

  2. Variability in time‐to‐fill measurements – this component is vital to computing velocity which is squared in the computations. A tipping‐bucket type flow measurement device would improve precision and repeatability. If the velocities are computed as too large (calling “stop” too soon) that too would lead to low friction factor values.

  3. Use of tabulated fluid properties not corrected for the actual temperature - this component would affect the computation of Re in the tables, and also produce off-theory friction factors. This source of error is probably the least significant, as the literature tabulated values change by less than 0.1% in the 5-degree temperature range near the laboratory temperature.

Despite these issues, the measured results follow the anticipated trend (in fact if the last two measurements are ignored the fitted lines would be parallel) reinforcing the theory of head loss in a pipe in laminar flow.

Interpretation

The experimentally obtained loss coefficients are summarized in the tables and displayed in the box-and-whisker plots below. For each fitting type, the black circles indicate the experimental means, while the red diamonds denote the commonly accepted literature values.

In four of the five fittings (mitre, elbow, contraction, and sweep), the experimental means exceed the corresponding literature values. The expansion fitting is the only case where the measured mean falls slightly below the published reference. This consistent bias suggests some combination of measurement uncertainty, local flow effects, or apparatus‐related losses not fully reflected in idealized literature values.

Although none of the literature values lie within the interquartile range (IQR) of the experimental distributions, most fall well within approximately three standard deviations of the observed data. In other words, while the central tendency of the experiments is shifted relative to published benchmarks, the spread of the data still encompasses the literature values within a statistically reasonable tolerance. Taken together, the results do not contradict established sources and generally support the existing literature, especially when accounting for typical variability in laboratory conditions.

Sources of Error#

  1. Flow rate determination by time-to-fill: The dominant source of experimental uncertainty arises from the use of time-to-fill measurements to estimate the volumetric flow rate. This value propagates through all subsequent calculations, including velocity, Reynolds number, friction factor, and fitting loss coefficients. Because the flow rates in the pipe‐loss experiment were below the stall threshold of the available flowmeters, no alternative measurement method was feasible.

  2. Approximation of fluid properties: Standard reference tables report density and viscosity at 5 °C intervals. The experimental fluid temperature was approximately 17 °C, which is not explicitly tabulated. The nearest tabulated values were adopted without interpolation. Given that density and viscosity typically vary by less than 0.1 percent over a 5 °C range, the resulting error is considered negligible relative to other uncertainties.

  3. Manometer reading resolution and vibration effects: The minimum practical reading increment on the manometer was 2 mm of water. The meniscus was of comparable thickness, and small pressure oscillations induced by the pump were visually apparent during measurement. Under these conditions, the resolution of the apparatus is at or near its practical limit. More precise measurements would require instrumentation such as pressure transducers integrated into a Wheatstone-bridge configuration with appropriate noise filtering.

  4. Transcription of recorded values: A minor source of potential error is the manual transfer of recorded measurements into the data tables. Although attention to accuracy was maintained, the possibility of transcription errors cannot be fully precluded.

Conclusion#

The experimental work successfully demonstrated that theoretical friction factors for laminar flow can be reproduced using small‐scale apparatus and relatively basic measurement techniques. Although exact numerical agreement with theoretical values was not achieved, the observed trends closely followed the expected behavior. When the identified sources of experimental uncertainty are considered, the results fall well within a reasonable range of agreement. These findings support the reliability of published friction factor correlations and indicate that laboratory‐scale validation is feasible even with modest instrumentation.

Similarly, the literature values for minor loss coefficients were effectively corroborated. Because such coefficients are empirically derived and inherently sensitive to geometry, connection type (e.g., threaded, flanged, or solvent‐welded), and material, some variability between measured and published values is expected. In this context, the experimental results are sufficiently close to the literature benchmarks to be considered confirmatory. The small physical scale of the fittings and limitations in flow measurement likely account for most of the observed deviations. An alternative interpretation is that the slight positive bias in experimental results reflects conservative reporting practices in the literature, intended to reduce the risk of under‐design in hydraulic systems.

Overall, the study provides credible experimental support for established friction factor relationships and minor loss coefficients. The outcomes reinforce the applicability of literature values while highlighting the importance of measurement resolution, apparatus scale, and experimental method when interpreting laboratory results.

# Prototype Functions

# Compute Area for a Circular Pipe
def area_circle(diameter):
    import math
    area_circle = (math.pi*diameter**2)/4.0
    return area_circle

# Compute Reynolds Number
def reynolds(velocity,diameter,kinematic):
    reynolds = velocity*diameter/kinematic
    return(reynolds)

# Compute Friction Factor given Headloss
def fricfact(length,diameter,velocity,gravity,headloss):
    fricfact = headloss*(diameter/length)*((4.0*gravity)/velocity**2)
    return(fricfact)

# Compute loss coefficient given headloss and velocity
def Kfitting(headloss,velocity,gravity):
    Kfitting=headloss/((velocity**2)/(2.0*gravity))
    return(Kfitting)
