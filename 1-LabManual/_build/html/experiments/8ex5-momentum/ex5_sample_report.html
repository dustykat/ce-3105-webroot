Laboratory 5 Sample Report#

Momentum Transfer in a Jet#

Objectives#

  • To demonstrate that the force on a vane is proportional to the rate of delivery of momentum

  • To show that you can predict the force on a vane from a combination of its surface shape and the properties of the jet directed at it

Safety considerations#

  1. Don’t drink the water!

  2. Don’t come to lab intoxicated!

  3. Don’t wear shorts, skirt.

  4. Don’t wear sandals; shoes or boots (closed toe, Crocks don’t count)

  5. What about electricity and water?

  6. What about moving water and your eyeballs?

Experimental Setup and Methods#

The two experimental configurations (flat-plate and hemispherical cup) are described in the sub-sections that follow:

Flat Plate Vane#

The arrangement of the system is shown in the photograph below for the flat plate condition.

Not shown is the RPi (external) mass flow meter, and the H1F meter (built-in).

The experimental procedure is to:

  1. Install the Vane

  2. Establish repeatable zero for the weight beam is balance

  3. Start the hydraulic bench and adjust flow (work from high flow to low flow)

  4. Move the jockey weight until the beam balances at the repeatable zero

    • Record distance, y, from the zero position

    • Record the flow rate using the hydraulic bench H1F readout

    • Record the flow rate using the PRi external flowmeter

  5. Change the hydraulic bench flow rate in relatively equal increments for 4 additional readings (attempt to get 5 different flow rates, including lowest possible to produce constant deflection

    • Record distance, y, from the zero position for each trial

    • Record the flow rate using the hydraulic bench H1F readout for each trial

    • Record the flow rate using the PRi external flowmeter readout for each trial

The generic reduction procedure is to:

  1. Convert flow rate to mass flow rate, \(\dot m~\frac{kg}{s}\), from \(\dot m = \rho ~ Q\)

  2. Calculate the jet velocity, \(u_j~\frac{m}{s}\), from \(u = \frac{4Q}{\pi d^2}\) where \(d\) is the nozzle diameter.

  3. Calculate the deflected velocity, \(u_0~\frac{m}{s}\) from \(u_0 = \sqrt{u_j^2 - 2gs}\) where \(s\) is the nozzle-to-vane distance. This velocity accounts for the conversion of kinetic energy to gravitational potential as the jet rises the distance \(s\) before impact with the vane.

  4. Calculate rate of delivery of momentum, \(\dot m u_0 ~ \frac{kg~m}{s}\)

  5. Calculate force, \(F~\frac{kg~m}{s^2}\). The values are obtained from the data table(s) \(F = \frac{M \cdot g \cdot (a + y)}{a}\), where \(y\) is the jockey weight distance from the repeatable zero, \(a\) is the pivot to vane attachment location, and \(M\) is the mass of the jockey weight.

  6. Create a plot of force, \(F\) (y-axis) vs. rate of delivery of momentum, \(\dot m u_0\)(x-axis)

  • Create a trendline for the data points

  • The slope of the trendline indicates the coefficient for theoretical force

Results and Discussion#

For each type of vane, the expected force can be estimated using the momentum equation. When we plot force versus momentum and fit a straight line, the slope of that line represents (\(1-cos(\beta)\)), where \(\beta\) iis the flow deflection angle, as shown in the sketch below:

Flat Plate

The plot of Force vs. Momentum for the flat plate is shown below with a fitted line in red. The script used to create the graph and fit the line is included in the appendix.

The slope of the line is 0.98. This corresponds to a deflection angle of \(\frac{\pi}{2.03}\), which is very close to the ideal value of \(\frac{\pi}{2}\).

Hemispherical Cup

The Force vs. Momentum data for the hemispherical cup is shown in the next plot, again with a red fitted line. The code used to generate the figure is also in the appendix.

For this case, the slope is 1.94. That corresponds to a deflection angle of \(\frac{\pi}{1.12}\), which is close to the theoretical value of \(\frac{\pi}{1}\).

Sources of Error#

There were several sources of error during the experiment:

  • Flow measurement issues: At low flow rates, the H1F flow meter sometimes showed zero even when flow was visible. The PRi mass flow meter was able to measure those flows. In most runs, the two meters agreed within about 2%, with the PRi reading slightly lower. A third method was not available to verify the actual flow rate.

  • Reading the jockey weight: There could be small errors when reading the mass position or making sure the system returned to the same zero point each time. This probably didn’t matter much at higher flows.

  • Flow instability: The flow was observed to fluctuate as the pump delivered pressure, which could add uncertainty to the force and flow readings.

Conclusion#

Overall, the experimental values matched the theoretical predictions closely. The method used in this lab could also be applied to vane shapes where the theoretical force is harder to calculate. The results show that it is possible to determine an effective deflection angle from measurements and use that information for design.

Appendix-I Python Analysis Scripts#

def mass_flow(rho,Q):
    '''function to return mass flow in kg/s
         rho == density kg/m^3
         Q == flow rate m^3/s
         user must make unit conversions in parameter list
    '''
    mass_flow = rho*Q  
    return(mass_flow)

def appliedF(mass,g,a,y):
    '''function to return applied force in Newtons
    '''
    appliedF=mass*g*(a+y)/a
    return(appliedF)
    
import math
# Data from tables
y_plate=[102,86,68,42,19]
q_plate=[35.5,28.4,27.2,22.6,17.3]
y_cup  =[188,140,90,50,20]
q_cup  =[33.1,29.8,24.3,18.2,12.6]

# Constants
rho      = 1000     #kg/m^3
g        = 9.81     #m/s^2
diameter = 0.010    #m  nozzle diameter
s        = 0.035    #m distance between nozzle-vane
Mass     = 600/1000 #kg the jockey mass
a        = 0.150    # m vane-pivot distance

# Calculations
# - allocate memory locations
mdot_plate = [] # mass flow plate
mdot_cup = []   # mass flow cup
vj_plate=[]     # jet velocity plate
vj_cup=[]       # jet velocity cup
vd_plate=[]     # deflection velocity plate
vd_cup=[]       # deflection velocity cup
mom_plate = []  # momentum entering at plate
mom_cup = []    # momentum entering at cup
f_plate = []
f_cup = []
# - populate vectors 
for i in range(len(y_plate)):
    mdot_plate.append(mass_flow(1000,q_plate[i]/1000/60))
    mdot_cup.append(mass_flow(1000,q_cup[i]/1000/60))
    vj_plate.append(4*(q_plate[i]/1000/60)/(math.pi*diameter**2))
    vj_cup.append(4*(q_cup[i]/1000/60)/(math.pi*diameter**2))
    vd_plate.append(math.sqrt(vj_plate[i]**2 - 2*g*s))
    vd_cup.append(math.sqrt(vj_cup[i]**2 - 2*g*s))
    mom_plate.append(mdot_plate[i]*vd_plate[i])
    mom_cup.append(mdot_cup[i]*vd_cup[i])
    f_plate.append(appliedF(Mass,g,a,y_plate[i]/1000))
    f_cup.append(appliedF(Mass,g,a,y_cup[i]/1000))
# - print results for tabulation
print("plate")
for i in range(len(y_plate)):
    print("|",i+1,"|",q_plate[i],"|",round(mdot_plate[i],3),"|",y_plate[i],"|"\
         ,round(vj_plate[i],3),"|",round(vd_plate[i],3),"|",round(mom_plate[i],3),"|",round(f_plate[i],3),"|")
print("cup")
for i in range(len(y_cup)):
    print("|",i+1,"|",q_cup[i],"|",round(mdot_cup[i],3),"|",y_cup[i],"|"\
         ,round(vj_cup[i],3),"|",round(vd_cup[i],3),"|",round(mom_cup[i],3),"|",round(f_cup[i],3),"|")

import numpy as np

# Fit a linear model: F = m*x + b
m, b = np.polyfit(mom_plate, f_plate, 1)
fit_line = m * np.array(mom_plate) + b

# Scatter plot of original data
plt.scatter(mom_plate, f_plate, label="$F(J)$ for the flat plate")

# Plot fitted line
plt.plot(mom_plate, fit_line,
         label=f"Fit: $F(J) = {m:.2f}J + {b:.2f}$",
         linestyle='-',color="red")

# Labels, title, legend, and grid
plt.xlabel("Momentum $J=\\dot{m}\\cdot u_0$ \n ${kg·m^2}/{s^2}$")
plt.ylabel("Force $F=\\frac{M\\cdot g \\cdot (a+y)}{a}$\n $(kg~m/s^2)$")
plt.title("Flat Plate Vane")
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True)
plt.savefig("plate_plot.png", dpi=300, bbox_inches="tight")
