3. Role of Engineering Experimentation#

The primary role of engineering experimentation is to progress from theory (dreamed up in our biological computers) to validation (confirm our theory is useful, and how accurate a predictor it may be).

Learning Objectives#

By the end of this session, students will be able to:

  • Explain the scientific method in historical and engineering contexts

  • Distinguish between physical laws, empirical models, and measured data

  • Apply model-based reasoning to experimental design

  • Use error analysis to evaluate the agreement between theory and experiment

Part 1: The Scientific Method ‚Äî Historical and Practical Application#

In this laboratory course, we repeatedly apply the scientific method as a process for testing and validating models of fluid behavior.

Each experiment will follow a workflow that mimics elements of the scientific method; the workflow summary is:

  1. Model Definition We begin by identifying the governing physical principles and equations that define the theoretical model (e.g., Bernoulli‚Äôs equation, momentum balance, or head loss formulas). In the experiments we are ultimately going to test the utility of the model to explain some real phenomenon. The definition will involve some derivation, sketching, and analysis like you have already done in CE 3305.

  2. Model Predictions Using known parameters and appropriate assumptions, we calculate what the model predicts should occur under the given experimental conditions. This component is conducted before conducting any experiments or making any measurements.

  3. Measurement and Observation Here is where we conduct the experiment, collecting data through direct measurement of key variables such as flow rate, pressure, or velocity.

  4. Comparison and Error Analysis We analyze how well the measured values align with the predicted results, using plots and error metrics such as percent error or RMS error to quantify agreement.

  5. Interpretation and Evaluation Finally, we reflect on the model‚Äôs performance. Where results diverge from theory, we consider the influence of experimental error, unmodeled effects, or invalid assumptions, and discuss whether the model should be accepted, rejected, or refined.

This workflow turns each experiment into a rigorous test of theory, helping you build both conceptual understanding and practical insight into fluid mechanics.

A Brief History of the Scientific Method#

Wikipedia: History of the Scientific Method presents a good discourse on the history of the ‚Äúmethod;‚Äù notable players of that history include:

  • Francis Bacon (1620s): Proposed structured observation and inductive reasoning. The Baconian method is the investigative method developed by Francis Bacon, one of the founders of modern science, and thus a first formulation of a modern scientific method. The method was put forward in Bacon‚Äôs book Novum Organum (1620), or ‚ÄòNew Method‚Äô, to replace the old methods put forward in Aristotle‚Äôs Organon. It influenced the early modern rejection of medieval Aristotelianism. He explicitly addressed causality in his writings on the subject.

  • Galileo Galilei: Used mathematical models to describe physical phenomena, and designed experiments to test them. Galileo Galilei played a pivotal role in shaping the modern scientific method by emphasizing experimentation, observation, and mathematical analysis. He developed a method that involved intuition, demonstration, and experimental verification to test hypotheses and build upon existing knowledge. Galileo‚Äôs work, particularly in physics and astronomy, demonstrated the power of combining observation, experimentation, and mathematical reasoning to understand the natural world

  • Isaac Newton: Developed universal laws that combined observation, theory, and prediction. Isaac Newton‚Äôs scientific method was a refined approach to scientific inquiry that emphasized observation, experimentation, and the use of mathematical reasoning to develop and test theories. He used a combination of theoretical reasoning and empirical observation to draw inferences and develop his groundbreaking theories of motion and gravitation.

Adaptation to Engineering#

The engineering design process is a structured approach to solving problems, building upon the principles of the scientific method but with a focus on practical application and creating tangible solutions. The primary tool is the model which relates the excitation-response behavior of the world to the phenomenon of interest. Designers can rely upon the model once it is demonstrated to be reliable and accurate - the point of engineering experimentation is usually to test the model (for applicability, reliability, and accuracy).

  • Theories (e.g., conservation laws) ‚Üí produce models.

  • Models ‚Üí generate predictions given specific inputs.

  • Experiments ‚Üí gather data to test how well predictions match reality.

Part 2: Reinforcement Activities#

Activity 1: Matching Models to Measurements#

Students are given:

  • 3 theoretical models:

    • Flow through an orifice

    • Pipe friction loss

    • Jet impact momentum

  • 3 sets of plotted or tabulated measurements

Task: Match each dataset to a model and identify likely causes of mismatch.

Activity 2 (Optional): Quick Error Propagation Exercise#

Given:

  • Model: \(Q = C_d A \sqrt{2gh} \)

  • Variables with uncertainties:

    • \(h = 0.75 \pm 0.01 \ \text{m}\)

    • \(A = 0.0025 \pm 0.0001 \ \text{m}^2\)

    • \( C_d = 0.62 \pm 0.02\)

Task: Compute \(Q\) and its uncertainty via propagation, compare with observed spread in measurements.

Part 3: Framework for Fluids Laboratory#

Every experiment in this course will follow a consistent model-testing cycle based on the scientific method.

Experimental Workflow:#

  1. Theory Review

    • Identify governing laws or assumptions

    • Examples: Bernoulli‚Äôs equation, momentum balance, continuity

  2. Model Prediction

    • Use the theoretical model to compute expected behavior

    • Ex: Predict pressure drop, velocity, force, etc.

  3. Measurement & Observation

    • Take experimental data using instruments and sensors

    • Document uncertainties

  4. Comparison & Validation

    • Plot: experimental vs. model values

    • Compute error metrics:

      • Percent error

      • Root-mean-square error (RMSE)

      • Residuals or correlation coefficient

  5. Conclusion

    • Does the model accurately predict behavior?

    • If not, why? Assumption failure or measurement limits?

Reinforcement Assignments#

Pre-Lab: Mini-Model Statement#

Before each lab, students submit:

  • A short statement of the governing model

  • A prediction of expected outcome

  • List of assumptions and what would indicate ‚Äúgood agreement‚Äù

Post-Lab: Comparison and Reflection#

Lab reports must include:

  • Explicit comparison between predicted and measured values

  • At least one error analysis method

  • Final reflection: Does the experiment support the model? Why or why not?

Closing Thoughts#

Engineering is not just about applying formulas‚Äîit‚Äôs about testing ideas against reality. The lab is not a place to ‚Äúget the right answer‚Äù‚Äîit‚Äôs a place to confront models with nature, and decide whether we believe our assumptions.

Structured Inquiry vs. ‚ÄúCook-and-Look‚Äù: Two Paths Through the Laboratory

Overview

Not all experiments are created equal. In engineering education, two styles of experimentation commonly emerge:

  • Scientific-method-directed experiments, where theory guides the inquiry, predictions are made, and results are critically evaluated to test a hypothesis or validate a model.

  • ‚ÄúCook-and-look‚Äù experiments, where a procedure is followed step-by-step with little theoretical framing or expectation, often leading to passive observation rather than active discovery.

Understanding the distinction is essential not only for academic success, but for cultivating the mindset of a practicing engineer.

Scientific-Method-Directed Experimentation

The characteristics of scientific-method directed inquiry are:

  • Begins with a hypothesis or model derived from theory

  • Predictions are made in advance

  • Experiments are designed to test, calibrate, or refute the model

  • Measurements are compared to expectations with quantitative rigor

  • Discrepancies lead to refinement of models and deeper understanding

Some noteable successes attributed to structured inquiry are:

  • Galileo‚Äôs inclined plane experiments (1600s) Tested mathematical relationships between acceleration, time, and distance, providing early evidence for the laws of motion.

  • Cavendish‚Äôs measurement of the gravitational constant (1797) Designed to quantify Newton‚Äôs law of universal gravitation‚Äînot merely observe attraction.

  • Prandtl‚Äôs boundary layer experiments (early 1900s) Theoretical analysis led to the design of experiments that revealed the structure of viscous flow, revolutionizing fluid mechanics.

Notable failures include:

  • Michelson‚ÄìMorley experiment (1887) Failed to detect the ‚Äúaether wind‚Äù predicted by classical physics. Though it produced a ‚Äúnull result,‚Äù it motivated a shift in thinking that led to Einstein‚Äôs theory of relativity. It is an example of a model built on faulty assumptions, hence the experiment could not validate the model, researchers modified assumptions over time until Einstein‚Äôs model(s) indeed could explain a particular behavior.

‚ÄúCook-and-Look‚Äù Experimentation

The characteristics of ‚Äúcook-and-look‚Äù directed inquiry are:

  • Follows a fixed set of steps like a recipe, often from a lab manual

  • No hypothesis or model-driven prediction is made

  • Data is collected without clear expectations

  • Emphasis is on procedure compliance, not interpretation

  • Often ends with, ‚ÄúThe results didn‚Äôt match, but we followed the steps.‚Äù

Some occasional successes attributed to cook-and-look are:

  • Alexander Fleming‚Äôs discovery of penicillin (1928) Accidental contamination of a petri dish revealed antibacterial effects‚Äînot predicted by theory, but recognized through observation.

  • Discovery of cosmic microwave background (Penzias and Wilson, 1965) Researchers were troubleshooting antenna noise and stumbled onto the remnant of the Big Bang‚Äîwithout initially understanding the implications.

These examples show that ‚Äúcook-and-look‚Äù can occasionally lead to breakthroughs, but only if the observer is prepared to recognize the unexpected and reframe the context.

More common are failures, where the results are of little value:

  • Countless teaching, research, and industrial laboratories where results are recorded without interpretation.

  • Industrial settings where procedures are followed, but underlying phenomena are misunderstood, leading to poor troubleshooting or design decisions

Bear in mind that cook-and-look experiments are totally appropriate for certain compliance purposes, in such instances the rote application of process is important to determine if something is to be certified to a standard or not. Arguably there is scientific method involved, because the standards to be met are the ‚Äúmodel‚Äù, and if the experiment fails, the component is inadequate.

This type of experimentation emphasizes verification over discovery, and the value lies in reproducibility and adherence to established thresholds rather than hypothesis generation. While it may lack the iterative curiosity of traditional scientific inquiry, it still involves rigorous observation, data collection, and comparison to a known benchmark. In this context, the ‚Äúmodel‚Äù is codified in standards documents‚Äîsuch as ASTM or ISO‚Äîand the experiment‚Äôs purpose is to determine conformance, not to explore new phenomena.

Why Scientific Method Matters in Engineering

Engineers do not merely measure‚Äîthey model, predict, and verify. Experiments are not recipes; they are tools to challenge assumptions, test applicability, and build confidence in our understanding of physical systems.

In this laboratory course, we reject the ‚Äúcook-and-look‚Äù mindset. Instead, we adopt a model-driven approach that values prediction, observation, comparison, and critical thinking. Your role is not to confirm the manual‚Äîit is to test nature and decide whether our models are worthy of your trust.

Mini-Model Statement (Pre-Lab)#

Before performing the experiment, complete the following:

1. Governing Model or Principle#

Briefly describe the physical law or empirical relationship being tested.

Example: ‚ÄúThis experiment is based on the Bernoulli equation, which relates pressure, velocity, and elevation along a streamline for inviscid flow.‚Äù

2. Model Equation(s)#

State the key equation(s) to be used for prediction, including definitions of all variables.

Example: \( p + \frac{1}{2} \rho V^2 + \rho g h = \text{constant}\)

3. Assumptions#

List the assumptions necessary for the model to apply.

Examples:

  • Steady flow

  • Incompressible fluid

  • Negligible viscous losses

4. Prediction of Behavior#

Based on the model and inputs, describe the expected behavior or result.

Example: ‚ÄúWe expect that increasing the velocity will decrease the pressure at the nozzle exit due to energy conservation.‚Äù

5. Evaluation Criteria#

State how agreement between theory and experiment will be assessed.

Example: ‚ÄúAgreement will be evaluated using percent error between the predicted and measured velocities. A deviation under 10% will be considered acceptable.‚Äù

Post-Lab Reflection Rubric#

Include a structured reflection as the final section of your lab report. Address each of the following categories. This reflection is worth 15 points and should demonstrate your ability to connect theory with observation.

1. Agreement with the Model (5 points)#

  • Clearly describe whether your measurements aligned with the model‚Äôs predictions.

  • Use graphs, tables, or error metrics (e.g., % error, RMSE) to support your conclusion.

Example: ‚ÄúThe measured head loss was within 8% of the predicted value for all trials, suggesting good agreement with the Darcy-Weisbach model.‚Äù

2. Explanation of Discrepancies (5 points)#

  • If results deviated from the model, identify possible reasons:

    • Instrument error

    • Violation of assumptions

    • Model oversimplification

  • Distinguish between random and systematic error where appropriate.

Example: ‚ÄúThe persistent underestimation of force may be due to unaccounted friction in the pulley system, violating the ideal assumption of lossless transfer.‚Äù

3. Implications and Next Steps (5 points)#

  • Reflect on the experiment‚Äôs value in validating, challenging, or refining the model.

  • Suggest follow-up measurements, improved procedures, or potential calibrations.

Example: ‚ÄúThis experiment suggests the need to include a correction factor for contraction losses when modeling short orifices under real-world conditions.‚Äù

Reflection Format Tips#

  • Use short paragraphs.

  • Refer to specific results or figures in your report.

  • Avoid vague conclusions like ‚ÄúIt worked‚Äù or ‚ÄúIt didn‚Äôt work.‚Äù

Total: 15 points
Scoring rubric will assess completeness, clarity, and thoughtfulness.

Example 1: Flow Measurement with a Venturi#

üî¨ Part I ‚Äî Mini-Model Statement (Pre-Lab)#

1. Governing Model or Principle#

This experiment is based on the Bernoulli equation, which states that for an incompressible, steady, inviscid flow along a streamline, the total mechanical energy is conserved. The pressure drop between the wide and narrow sections of a Venturi meter can be related to the flow velocity.

2. Model Equation(s)#

\(\Delta p = \frac{1}{2} \rho \left( V_2^2 - V_1^2 \right) \)

Where:

  • \(\Delta p\) = pressure difference between upstream and throat

  • \(\rho\) = fluid density

  • \(V_1, V_2\) = velocities at inlet and throat (related via continuity)

From continuity: \(Q = A_1 V_1 = A_2 V_2\)

3. Assumptions#

  • Steady flow

  • Incompressible fluid (water)

  • No energy loss due to friction or turbulence

  • Accurate pressure measurements via manometer

4. Prediction of Behavior#

We expect the pressure at the throat to be lower than at the inlet due to increased velocity. Using measured pressure drop and known cross-sectional areas, we can calculate discharge \(Q\) and compare it to direct volumetric measurements.

5. Evaluation Criteria#

We will compare the discharge calculated from the Bernoulli-based model to the flow rate measured by time-to-fill a graduated tank. Agreement within ¬±5% will be considered acceptable.

Part II ‚Äî Post-Lab Reflection#

1. Agreement with the Model (5 pts)#

The model-based flow rate was within 4.2% of the directly measured volumetric flow rate for all trials. A comparison plot (Figure 3) shows a strong correlation (R¬≤ = 0.993) between predicted and measured values.

2. Explanation of Discrepancies (5 pts)#

Minor discrepancies likely arose from:

  • Inexact manometer readings due to parallax

  • Slight turbulence or energy loss not accounted for in the model

  • Assumption of ideal flow conditions may not be fully valid in the experimental apparatus

3. Implications and Next Steps (5 pts)#

This experiment confirms that the Bernoulli-based model provides a valid estimate of flow rate in a Venturi meter under controlled conditions. Future experiments could investigate how accuracy degrades with increasing turbulence or cavitation, or apply a discharge coefficient \( C_d\) to improve model fit.

Total: 15/15

üß™ Pre/Post-Lab Worksheet: Model-Based Experimentation#

üî¨ Part I ‚Äî Mini-Model Statement (Pre-Lab)#

1. Governing Model or Principle#

This experiment tests the empirical model for head loss due to local disturbances in pipe systems (elbows, valves, contractions). The model is based on energy loss per unit weight using the minor loss coefficient \( K\).

2. Model Equation(s)#

\( h_L = K \cdot \frac{V^2}{2g} \)

Where:

  • \( h_L\) = head loss across fitting

  • \( K\) = loss coefficient (dimensionless, provided in literature)

  • \( V\) = velocity through fitting

  • \( g\) = gravitational acceleration

3. Assumptions#

  • Steady, incompressible flow

  • Fully developed velocity profile

  • Fittings are ideal representations (sharp angles, no roughness)

  • Flow velocity is uniform and measured accurately

4. Prediction of Behavior#

Using literature \( K\) values and measured velocities, we will predict \( h_L\) and compare it to observed pressure drops measured with manometers. Agreement within ¬±10% is expected.

5. Evaluation Criteria#

We will calculate the theoretical \( h_L\) and compare it to measured values. Discrepancies will be assessed using percent error and qualitative examination of flow regimes (e.g., cavitation, swirl).

üìä Part II ‚Äî Post-Lab Reflection#

1. Agreement with the Model (5 pts)#

Results varied significantly depending on the fitting. For a standard elbow, predicted and measured losses were within 7%, but for a sudden contraction, the measured loss was nearly 40% higher than predicted. The straight gate valve also showed large scatter.

2. Explanation of Discrepancies (5 pts)#

Discrepancies are likely due to:

  • Uncertainty in exact geometry and roughness of fittings vs. textbook assumptions

  • Unsteady flow and local turbulence not accounted for by static \( K\) values

  • Measurement error from manometers in low-head-loss scenarios

  • Flow separation or secondary flows in the contraction that violate assumed velocity profiles

3. Implications and Next Steps (5 pts)#

This experiment demonstrates that empirical \( K\) values may not generalize well without calibration. Real-world systems likely require field measurement or correction factors. Future work should include:

  • Repetition with digital pressure sensors

  • Calibration of system-specific \( K\) values using best-fit curves

  • CFD modeling of complex flow structures in contractions
